My Story

I grew up in San Diego — which means surfing wasn't really a choice, it was just what you did on weekends. I caught my first real wave at Ocean Beach when I was around twelve, wiped out immediately, and was completely hooked.

Living in Hawaii for college changed everything. Suddenly I had access to some of the most powerful, consistent surf on the planet. I went from a weekend cruiser to someone who would wake up at 5am to check the buoy reports before class.

Since then, surfing has taken me to Costa Rica and Bali — two places that completely reshaped how I think about waves, travel, and what it means to be present in the water. There is nothing that quiets your mind quite like a 6-foot set on the horizon coming straight at you.

Pipeline, North Shore — where I want to be every morning.

Favorite Spots

Four places, four completely different surfing experiences. Here's how I'd rank them and what makes each one special:

  1. Bali, Indonesia
    • Uluwatu is one of the most perfect left-handers on Earth
    • Warm water, consistent swell, and world-class barrels
    • Best season: May through September (dry season)
    • Watch out for: shallow reef, sea urchins, crowds at peak times
  2. North Shore, Oahu, Hawaii
    • Pipeline, Sunset, Waimea — legendary breaks within a few miles
    • Winter swells from the North Pacific create monstrous waves
    • Best season: November through February
    • Watch out for: serious surf, not for beginners in winter
  3. Nosara, Costa Rica
    • Playa Guiones is one of the most consistent beach breaks in Central America
    • Warm water, friendly locals, and a laid-back surf culture
    • Best season: April through October (rainy season = best swell)
    • Watch out for: rip currents when swell gets big
  4. San Diego, California
    • Black's Beach, Ocean Beach, and Windansea are my home breaks
    • Year-round surfing with clean point breaks and beach breaks
    • Best season: Fall — Santa Ana winds create offshore conditions
    • Watch out for: cold water (wetsuit required most of the year)

What You Need to Get Started

A lot of beginners overthink the gear. Here's the honest breakdown of what actually matters when you're starting out:

  • Longboard or foam board — stability beats looking cool every time
  • Wetsuit — match the thickness to your water temperature
  • Leash — keeps the board attached so it doesn't hit other surfers
  • Wax — you need traction or you'll slide straight off
  • Reef-safe sunscreen — you will be out there longer than you think

Most importantly: take a lesson before paddling out on your own. Understanding rip currents, surf etiquette, and how to read a break will save you a lot of embarrassment and keep you safe.

The right board makes all the difference.
